\begin{frame}{This paper}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fact 1.} Hedge funds are the marginal intermediaries between sovereign cash and futures markets, and the trade they run is not the modeled basis trade.
    \item \textbf{Fact 2.} The trade concentrates in cheap off-the-run bonds and is duration neutral but convexity exposed.
    \item \textbf{Fact 3.} Three dealers finance more than half of it.
    \item \textbf{Experiment.} Dealer funding shocks and the April 2025 volatility shock reveal the fragility.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Can the Treasury kill the basis trade? Steven Kelly, Without Warning, February 2024}
  \small
  \keypoint{Yes, gently. By issuing more bills the Treasury would serve the money demand that currently funds the trade and crowd it out without any regulatory crackdown.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The argument in steps}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The trade is repo funded. Cash invested in Treasury repo would rather sit in bills, so basis traders are manufacturing short term money assets out of long term bonds.
    \item The repo footprint therefore measures untapped structural demand at the short end. The Treasury can issue substantially more bills into it with limited indigestion, and direct issuance crowds out the trade.
    \item The futures side adjusts on its own. Asset managers hold long futures to close the duration gap between credit heavy portfolios and Treasury heavy benchmarks, and a bill heavier index shrinks that gap and the futures demand with it.
    \item The cost is small for the safest issuer. Rollover risk is limited, short debt already acts as the shock absorber in crises, and forgone episodes of negative term premium are the price of less blowup risk.
    \item Historically the Treasury only termed out its debt while the Fed ran QE, and the basis trade grew as QE unwound.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \takeaway{Issuance composition is a policy lever on the size of the trade, a determinant that sits upstream of the funding architecture and fragility we study.}
\end{frame}

\section{Risk and return of the trade}
\begin{frame}{Risk and return of the trade}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Positions are duration matched but leave a convexity gap that widens with rate volatility and the term premium. \todohl{show this graphically}
    \item Returns decompose into convergence, carry and specialness net of financing and margin costs.

  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Fragility through the dealer funding channel}
\begin{frame}{Fragility through the dealer funding channel}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Changes in basis positions are caused by the supply of dealer financing, not by the funds' own capital or views.
    \item The identification problem is that a fund cutting positions looks the same whether the fund chose to or its dealer pulled the financing.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}\quad{\small\color{slate} the Khwaja and Mian design}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item We observe the same fund borrowing from several dealers at the same time.
    \item Take a shock that hits one dealer and not the others, recurring year end balance sheet contractions for power and April 2025 for salience.
    \item Fund$\times$time fixed effects absorb the fund's capital, beliefs and redemptions, so a differential contraction at the shocked dealer is financing supply.
    \item Trace it forward. Does the fund replace the financing elsewhere or does the position get cut, and which bonds go first?
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \takeaway{With three dealers supplying half the capital, a shock to one dealer is a shock to the market, not a rounding error.}
\end{frame}

\section{Fragility through the volatility shock}
\begin{frame}{Fragility through the volatility shock}{Cross market transmission, a fund level test in quantities}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The same funds run both a UST and an EGB basis book, so losses in one book force adjustment in the other through shared capital and margin.
    \item April 2025 is the natural experiment, the tariff shock realized the convexity gap in the UST book.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The unit is the fund. Treatment is the fund's ex ante UST exposure and the outcome is how its European positions change.
    \item Did exposed funds cut more than other funds in the same bond and week, with bond$\times$time fixed effects?
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \takeaway{Same logic as the dealer channel with the fund as the node, separating contagion through common funds from contagion through common dealers.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Fragility through the volatility shock}{The spillover result, a bond level test in prices}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item What funds sell at the fund level matters only if it aggregates into bond level prices, the step from funds sold to prices moved.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The unit is the bond. Treatment is the bond's pre shock exposure to affected funds and the outcome is its price and liquidity.
    \item Did exposed bonds show wider bases, higher yields or worse liquidity than otherwise similar bonds?
    \item If funds adjusted but prices did not move, amplification is a null and a paragraph rather than a section.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Implications}
  \begin{itemize}

  \end{itemize}
