\begin{frame}{This paper}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fact 1.} Hedge funds are the marginal intermediaries between sovereign cash and futures markets, and the trade they run is not the modeled basis trade.
    \item \textbf{Fact 2.} The trade concentrates in cheap off-the-run bonds and is duration neutral but convexity exposed.
    \item \textbf{Fact 3.} Three dealers finance more than half of it.
    \item \textbf{Experiment.} Dealer funding shocks and the April 2025 volatility shock reveal the fragility.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Can the Treasury kill the basis trade? Steven Kelly, Blog post, February 2024}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{Tilting issuance toward bills (short-term instruments) would serve the money demand that funds the basis trade and crowd the trade out, no regulatory crackdown needed.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The basis trade connects three players with different preferences.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Money market funds want short safe money like assets. They would prefer bills but bills are scarce, so they lend cash in repo instead.
    \item Asset managers want long duration without funding bond purchases. They close their duration gap to a Treasury heavy benchmark with long futures.
    \item Hedge funds buy the Treasury and split it. They use the bond in repo which satisfies MMFs. Then they use that cash to go long more Treasuries which they can use to deliver into the futures.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{More bills unwind both legs at once.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Bills serve the money demand of MMFs directly, so cash migrates from repo to bills and the funding and carry of the trade dry up.
    \item A bill heavier index has less duration, so the benchmark gap and the futures demand of asset managers shrink with it.
    \item The cost to the Treasury is small since rollover risk is limited for the safest issuer and bills already absorb crisis issuance.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Improving the standing repo facility, Steven Kelly, Blog post, August 2022}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The SRF backstops lost repo funding but not falling collateral prices, so the Fed should lend against the hedged cash and futures package to keep basis traders in their positions during a dash for cash.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The SRF only fixes one of the two ways funding can break.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If repo refuses to roll or rolls at worse terms, the SRF substitutes well. It lends at the top of the policy rate range with a normal haircut.
    \item If a dash for cash pushes down the price of the bond itself, the SRF lends against that lower market price. The funding gap stays open and the Fed ends up buying Treasuries outright, as in March 2020.
    \item Access is limited to 35 dealers and banks. Everyone else depends on those members passing liquidity through their finite balance sheets.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{This matters for hedge funds because they are the marginal liquidity providers.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item In 2020 basis traders were long off the run Treasuries and short futures. The dash for cash pushed cash prices down while futures rallied, so both legs demanded liquidity at once.
    \item Forced liquidation by these funds works against monetary policy. Keeping them in their positions can be policy optimal.
    \item A matched position is safer collateral than the bond alone, so the Fed could lend more with less risk. The Bear Stearns rescue already provides the authority and the precedent.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Margin leverage and vulnerabilities in US Treasury futures, Avalos and Sushko, BIS Quarterly Review Box, September 2023}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The futures leg adds a second layer of leverage through initial margin. Margin hikes mechanically force deleveraging, so futures leverage is also worth monitoring.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The futures leg carries its own leverage.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Futures positions require initial margin at the clearinghouse. Leverage is the contract value over the margin, so 100 dollars of futures on 20 dollars of margin means five times leverage.
    \item This leverage stood near 175 times for five year and 120 times for ten year futures before the pandemic.
    \item When volatility rises the clearinghouse raises margins. Traders must post more cash or close positions, so deleveraging is mechanical.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{Margin hikes preceded both Treasury stress episodes.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Margins were hiked shortly before September 2019 and March 2020.
    \item Cash prices then outpaced futures and the basis inverted, which rewarded further unwinding. The spiral fed itself.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Financial stability report Section 5, Bank of England, December 2023}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The UST basis trade is among the key leverage vulnerabilities in market based finance, and the channel to the UK runs through the same hedge funds being active in Treasuries and gilts.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The basis trade sits on the FPC vulnerability list.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item In normal times the trade keeps cash and futures aligned and improves liquidity. The concern is only the leveraged unwind in stress.
    \item The list also names jump to illiquidity risk. Liquidity rests on finite intermediation capacity, so in stress it does not fade gradually but gaps once that capacity is exhausted.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{The UK angle is spillovers, not a domestic copy of the trade.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item There is no direct counterpart in the gilt market. UK asset managers lever through repo and swaps directly rather than through futures.
    \item Spillovers can still reach the UK through the same hedge funds trading both Treasuries and gilts and through disruption of the UST market itself.
    \item The Bank prefers backstop lending to NBFIs against good collateral over asset purchases, but the 2022 gilt episode showed lending can be too slow to stop a fire sale.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Infamous hedge fund trade draws fresh scrutiny as debt battle rages on, Bloomberg, May 2023}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The debt ceiling adds a stress channel. A default or downgrade impairs the Treasury collateral itself, and brinkmanship alone can push up repo rates against crowded one way positions.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The trade rests on its collateral being risk free.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Repo funding is secured by the Treasuries. A default or downgrade lowers collateral values, which raises margins and cuts borrowing capacity across the market plumbing.
    \item Even without a default, prolonged brinkmanship can surge repo rates and squeeze carry.
    \item Much of the repo is bilateral and outside any CCP, which the OFR calls a regulatory blind spot.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{The players and the counterpoint.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The active funds are largely the same as in 2020, Citadel, Millennium, ExodusPoint and Capula.
    \item The counterpoint in the article is that funds absorb the futures demand of asset managers and buy cash bonds while the Fed shrinks its portfolio, so they provide liquidity someone has to supply.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Griffin says scrutiny of the basis trade is utterly beyond me, Bloomberg, October 2023}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The industry defense. Basis traders provide liquidity and fund the Treasury market efficiently, saving taxpayers tens of basis points, which is billions of dollars a year.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The argument and its counterpoint in the same article.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Griffin says the systemic risk framing of the SEC and the Fed misses the funding cost the trade saves the taxpayer.
    \item Gensler replies that the generous prime brokerage funding behind such trades is the biggest source of risk in the financial system.
    \item Citadel ran a large basis portfolio that lost money in March 2020. Paul Tudor Jones says his basis book was in extreme duress and the entrance of the Fed into the market bailed it out.
    \item The defense therefore comes with a caveat. The liquidity provision held up in 2020 only because the Fed stepped in.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Treasury futures positions across investor types I, TBAC presentation, January 2024}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{\textit{Asset managers} are structurally long futures because futures finance credit overweights while keeping benchmark duration. Regulation and accounting push them toward futures instead of repo.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The long futures position is not a rate view.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Active managers are judged against an index that is heavy in Treasuries. Credit yields more than Treasuries, so they overweight credit to beat the index.
    \item They fund the credit by selling Treasuries. That leaves the portfolio with less interest rate risk than the index, so they buy Treasury futures to restore the duration. Futures need no cash.
    \item The lower the duration of the credit, the more futures are needed. A floating rate CLO has none at all, so its full Treasury replacement is bought in futures.
    \item Repo would be more precise. The manager would keep the exact bond and borrow cash against it. But 40 Act rules cap repo, most separate accounts ban it, and repo interest inflates published expense ratios, so futures win.
    \item With so many managers long, futures trade rich to cash bonds. Holding them costs about 45 basis points per year. Every major credit sector earns more, so managers pay it willingly.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Treasury futures positions across investor types II, TBAC presentation, January 2024}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{\textit{Hedge fund} shorts are the mirror of asset manager longs. The basis premium is the fee asset managers pay for their futures demand, and hedge funds implement the trade well beyond the CTD.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{The short side is structural facilitation, not a view.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Opposing directional views cannot explain positions that track each other this closely. Hedge funds accommodate the structural futures demand of asset managers.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{Implementation reaches beyond the cheapest to deliver.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item CTD bonds usually yield less than the fitted curve implies. Their delivery role and liquidity make them expensive, just like on the run bonds, so funds implement the trade with cheaper off the run bonds.
    \item Dealers reported large off the run liquidations by relative value funds in March 2020, confirming the broader implementation.
    \item The proposed monitoring list includes term repo spreads, margin changes and the concentration of funding sources in bilateral repo.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Hedge fund Treasury exposures, repo and margining, Banegas and Monin, Fed note, September 2023}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{Treasury exposures and repo borrowing concentrate in about 50 highly leveraged funds that mostly borrow at zero haircut. A 200 basis point haircut floor would cut their leverage significantly.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Concentration and zero haircuts go together.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The top 50 funds hold 83 percent of hedge fund Treasury exposure and 89 percent of repo borrowing. Their balance sheet leverage is three times that of the average fund.
    \item 74 percent of repo borrowing carries a zero or negative haircut, so lenders finance the full position and funds put up almost no capital.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{A haircut floor bites through profitability, not feasibility.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item A 200 basis point floor would require 12 billion of extra capital. Most funds could cover it from cash, so the trade would not stop.
    \item But leverage halves, so cash-futures spreads must double to keep returns the same, or funds cut borrowing.
    \item The tradeoff is more loss absorption in stress against wider spreads and thinner liquidity in normal times.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature}{Hedge funds favorite US bond trade is sputtering, Bloomberg, July 2026}
  \footnotesize
  \keypoint{The basis trade is shrinking because the forces that create the premium are reversing. Futures demand cools, dealers return as competitors, issuance tilts toward bills and the Fed stopped shrinking its balance sheet.}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Each driver reverses a mechanism from the earlier slides.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Asset managers trimmed their long futures as the rate outlook flipped toward hikes, so the futures premium the trade harvests shrinks.
    \item Deregulation eased the supplementary leverage ratio and dealers rebuilt record Treasury holdings. A dealer long bonds and short futures runs the same economics and competes the spread away.
    \item Issuance tilts toward bills and the Fed no longer shrinks its balance sheet, so less long duration needs private absorption.
    \item Crowding does the rest. More capital chasing the basis compresses returns until entry stops.
  \end{itemize}
  \smallskip
  \textbf{The stability reading cuts both ways.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item With less hedge fund leverage and more diverse participants, a simultaneous unwind becomes less likely.
    \item But the market has leaned on funds for liquidity, so their retreat tests who provides it instead.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Fragility through the dealer funding channel}
\begin{frame}{Fragility through the dealer funding channel}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Changes in basis positions are caused by the supply of dealer financing, not by the funds' own capital or views.
    \item The identification problem is that a fund cutting positions looks the same whether the fund chose to or its dealer pulled the financing.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}\quad{\small\color{slate} the Khwaja and Mian design}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item We observe the same fund borrowing from several dealers at the same time.
    \item Take a shock that hits one dealer and not the others, recurring year end balance sheet contractions for power and April 2025 for salience.
    \item Fund$\times$time fixed effects absorb the fund's capital, beliefs and redemptions, so a differential contraction at the shocked dealer is financing supply.
    \item Trace it forward. Does the fund replace the financing elsewhere or does the position get cut, and which bonds go first?
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \takeaway{With three dealers supplying half the capital, a shock to one dealer is a shock to the market, not a rounding error.}
\end{frame}

\section{Fragility through the volatility shock}
\begin{frame}{Fragility through the volatility shock}{Cross market transmission, a fund level test in quantities}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The same funds run both a UST and an EGB basis book, so losses in one book force adjustment in the other through shared capital and margin.
    \item April 2025 is the natural experiment, the tariff shock realized the convexity gap in the UST book.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The unit is the fund. Treatment is the fund's ex ante UST exposure and the outcome is how its European positions change.
    \item Did exposed funds cut more than other funds in the same bond and week, with bond$\times$time fixed effects?
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \takeaway{Same logic as the dealer channel with the fund as the node, separating contagion through common funds from contagion through common dealers.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Fragility through the volatility shock}{The spillover result, a bond level test in prices}
  \textbf{Hypothesis}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item What funds sell at the fund level matters only if it aggregates into bond level prices, the step from funds sold to prices moved.
  \end{itemize}
  \medskip
  \textbf{Test}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The unit is the bond. Treatment is the bond's pre shock exposure to affected funds and the outcome is its price and liquidity.
    \item Did exposed bonds show wider bases, higher yields or worse liquidity than otherwise similar bonds?
    \item If funds adjusted but prices did not move, amplification is a null and a paragraph rather than a section.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Implications}
\begin{frame}{Implications}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The risk is not just directional exposure but convexity and funding concentration in illiquid bonds.
    \item Current NBFI monitoring does not measure this, with consequences for the transparency and leverage debates.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
